\section{Experiments}
\label{section:exp}

All experiments use the synthetic corpus of \S\ref{sec:method:data}: training
samples are generated on the fly, and we evaluate on a held-out, pre-rendered
$1{,}000$-sample validation set (used for checkpoint selection) and an analogous
$1{,}000$-sample test set. Unless noted, the decoder, loss, optimiser, and
$30$-epoch budget are held fixed at the values in \S\ref{sec:method:train}; only
the factor under study varies. We organise the study around three questions:
which visual representation and decoder actually solve the task
(\S\ref{sec:exp:arch}), how the two paradigms scale with data
(\S\ref{sec:exp:scaling}), and whether the model transfers to real photographs
(\S\ref{sec:exp:sim2real}). \S\ref{sec:exp:deploy} describes the deployed demo.

\subsection{Which representation learns pitch?}
\label{sec:exp:arch}

We hold the decoder, loss, and data fixed and vary only the visual backbone and
the decoding paradigm, at $100{,}000$ training samples. Table~\ref{table:arch}
reports the result and Figure~\ref{fig:val_curves} the corresponding
autoregressive validation curves.

\paragraph{Findings.} Under the AR decoder, the pretrained \texttt{vit} encoder
solves the task almost perfectly ($\mathrm{SER}=0.0029$, $99.85\%$ pitch), while
the from-scratch \texttt{resnet} encoder learns rhythm only partially and
\textbf{structurally fails to learn pitch} (${\approx}0\%$): its pitch
cross-entropy is pinned at the ``\texttt{NULL}-only'' floor, i.e.\ it is correct
on non-note positions but uninformative on notes. A translation-equivariant CNN
that pools image height into one token per column discards the \emph{absolute
vertical position} of a notehead---which is exactly what pitch is on a staff. This
failure proved invariant to learning-rate sweeps, to replacing height
mean-pooling with a height-flatten, to higher vertical resolution, and to an
explicit learned vertical positional embedding, which is why the natural
conclusion was that the convolutional encoder simply cannot represent pitch.

The CTC row refutes that conclusion. Feeding the \emph{same} column-pooled ResNet
features to a BiLSTM\,+\,per-head CTC decoder reaches a \emph{perfect}
$\mathrm{SER}=0.0000$ with $100\%$ pitch and rhythm. The encoder was never the
bottleneck; the AR failure was a joint property of monotonic, one-column-per-symbol
cross-attention over $1$-D features. CTC's flexible alignment plus the BiLSTM's
bidirectional context let each column read the staff-line geometry of its
neighbours as a vertical reference frame, recovering pitch that AR could not.

\paragraph{Findings.} The two paradigms occupy qualitatively different
sample-efficiency regimes. CRNN+CTC reaches usable transcription already at
$5{,}000$ samples (val SER $0.067$, pitch $97.1\%$) and is within $10^{-3}$ of
perfect SER from $50{,}000$ upward. ViT-AR instead exhibits a sharp data
threshold: at ${\le}20{,}000$ samples its pitch head fails to learn at all (pitch
${\le}3.7\%$, val SER stuck near $0.9$, the regime where free-running generation
degenerates into runaway sequences), then snaps to val SER $0.0318$ / pitch
$97.5\%$ at $50{,}000$, close to its $100{,}000$-sample asymptote.

This contradicts the common expectation that ImageNet-pretrained features
dominate the low-data regime. The pretrained ViT prior carries no music-notation
structure: pitch depends on a notehead's absolute vertical position on the staff,
a cue ImageNet pretraining does not reward. Combined with AR's one-column-per-symbol
alignment, the pitch head needs far more data before it escapes the
\texttt{NULL}-only floor, whereas CRNN+CTC's bidirectional context and flexible
alignment make per-symbol learning proportionately more sample-efficient. (We
report a single seed per cell; the ViT-AR threshold may shift with seed and data
loader settings, but the order-of-magnitude gap is robust---see
\S\ref{section:discussion}.)

\subsection{Sim-to-real transfer}
\label{sec:exp:sim2real}

The deterministic generator lets us photograph the \emph{same} val/test samples
with a phone and score predictions quantitatively, since each photo pairs $1$:$1$
by filename with its synthetic ground-truth labels. The clean-trained \texttt{vit}
model collapses on these photos (Table~\ref{table:external}, top): test SER jumps
from $0.003$ (clean) to $0.634$ and pitch from $99.8\%$ to $32.5\%$. We then
warm-start from that checkpoint and fine-tune for $3$ epochs (LR $5\times10^{-5}$)
under a \texttt{photo} augmentation profile that adds, in order, native-resolution
lighting/degradation (brightness, gamma, shadows, blur, noise, JPEG) and, after
padding to the $224^2$ canvas, geometric warps (small rotation, shear,
perspective). Fine-tuning more than halves the error (test SER $0.258$, pitch
$64.5\%$) while clean validation barely regresses.

\paragraph{What in the augmentation matters?} A leave-one-out ablation
(Table~\ref{table:ablation}) drops each augmentation group in turn; each variant
is selected on the external \emph{val} split and reported on external \emph{test}.
Removing the geometric group alone reverts photo performance almost to baseline
(SER $0.605$, pitch $0.331$), a swing of $+0.347$ SER / $-0.314$ pitch---far
larger than dropping lighting ($+0.068$ / $-0.082$) or degradation ($+0.015$ /
$-0.011$). A control that fine-tunes with the \texttt{default} (non-photo) profile
matches the baseline on photos, confirming the gain comes from the augmentation,
not the extra training steps. The geometric group's dominance pinpoints the
sim-to-real gap as camera-angle skew causing vertical mis-registration of
noteheads---consistent with the residual pitch errors being mostly
``same octave, wrong step.''

\subsection{Deployment}
\label{sec:exp:deploy}

The full pipeline---inference, token-to-Jianpu mapping, and optional re-engraving
of the predicted score---is packaged as an interactive web demo. A user uploads or
selects a staff image; the app runs the \texttt{vit} model, renders aligned Jianpu
(continuous beams within a beat, octave dots), and can re-engrave the predicted
stream for visual comparison. Both the clean baseline and the photo fine-tuned
weights are selectable, so the sim-to-real improvement is directly observable on
user-supplied photographs.
